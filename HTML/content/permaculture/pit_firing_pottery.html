Pit Firing Pottery: A Traditional Ceramic Technique

Introduction to Pit Firing

Pit firing is one of the oldest methods of firing ceramics, dating back thousands of years. This technique involves firing pottery in a pit dug into the ground, using wood, animal dung, and other combustible materials to create unique, rustic finishes.

Cultural Significance: In Nepal and many indigenous cultures worldwide, pit firing is not just a pottery technique, but a deeply spiritual and communal process that connects artisans to traditional craftsmanship.

Materials and Preparation

Clay and Pottery Preparation

  • Low-fire clay body
  • Burnishing or terra sigillata for surface finish
  • Unfired pottery pieces
  • Leather-hard or bone-dry ceramics

Firing Materials

  • Dried wood
  • Animal dung
  • Sawdust
  • Leaves and small branches
  • Salt or copper carbonate (for color effects)
  • Combustible materials like seaweed or horse hair
Important Preparation: Pottery must be completely dry before pit firing. Any moisture can cause cracking or explosive breaking during the firing process.

Pit Preparation and Site Selection

  1. Choose a flat, open area away from flammable structures
  2. Dig a pit approximately:
    • Depth: 30-60 cm (12-24 inches)
    • Width: 60-90 cm (24-36 inches)
    • Length: Varies based on number of pieces
  3. Clear the pit of any debris or rocks
  4. Create a stable, level bottom
Traditional Nepalese Approach: In rural areas, potters often choose sites near rivers or in open fields, utilizing natural windbreaks and accessible materials.

Pottery Arrangement and Protection

Placement Techniques

  • Create a base layer of combustible materials:
    • Sawdust
    • Dried leaves
    • Small twigs
  • Carefully place pottery pieces:
    • Ensure pieces do not touch each other
    • Use supports like broken pottery shards
    • Protect delicate areas with additional combustible materials
  • Layer additional combustible materials between and around pieces
  • Optional: Wrap pieces in nichrome wire or copper wire for interesting surface effects

Decorative Techniques

  • Sprinkle salt for white flashing effects
  • Add copper carbonate for green/blue tones
  • Use horse hair or feathers for unique carbon trapping

Firing Process

  1. Build a gradual fire:
    • Start with small twigs and leaves
    • Slowly add larger pieces of wood
    • Maintain steady, moderate heat
  2. Allow fire to burn for 4-6 hours:
    • Maintain consistent temperature
    • Avoid sudden temperature changes
  3. Let pit cool naturally:
    • Do not disturb for 12-24 hours
    • Protect from wind and rain
Safety Precautions:
  • Keep fire extinguisher nearby
  • Have water source available
  • Clear surrounding area of flammable materials
  • Work in a well-ventilated area
  • Wear protective clothing and gloves

Post-Firing Care

  • Allow pieces to cool completely
  • Gently remove from pit
  • Clean with soft brush
  • Optional: Apply wax or oil for protection and shine
  • Inspect for any damage or fragility
Traditional Finishing: In many Nepalese communities, pottery is often sealed with natural oils or local beeswax to enhance durability and appearance.

Potential Challenges and Solutions

  • Cracking:
    • Ensure pottery is completely dry
    • Use even, slow heating
    • Avoid direct flame contact
  • Uneven Coloration:
    • Distribute combustible materials evenly
    • Experiment with placement and materials
  • Structural Weakness:
    • Use appropriate clay body
    • Avoid thin or uneven walls
    • Support delicate areas during firing
