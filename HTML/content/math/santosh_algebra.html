Profit and Loss in Math

Profit and Loss are basic financial concepts used in business and everyday transactions. They help determine whether a business or individual gains or loses money in a transaction.

Profit

When the selling price (SP) of an item is greater than its cost price (CP), the seller makes a profit.

Formula:

Profit = Selling Price - Cost Price

Profit Percentage:

Profit% = (Profit / Cost Price) ├Ч 100

Loss

When the selling price (SP) of an item is less than its cost price (CP), the seller incurs a loss.

Formula:

Loss = Cost Price - Selling Price

Loss Percentage:

Loss% = (Loss / Cost Price) ├Ч 100

Selling Price and Cost Price Formulas

Selling Price when profit is given:

SP = CP + Profit

Selling Price when loss is given:

SP = CP - Loss

These systems are fundamental to both theoretical mathematics and practical applications across various fields. In the simplest case, a system of two linear equations in two variables (x and y) represents two lines in a coordinate plane. The solution to such a system is the point (or points) where these lines intersect, representing values that satisfy both equations simultaneously. The nature of solutions in linear systems falls into three categories: unique solution (one intersection point), no solution (parallel lines), or infinitely many solutions (identical lines). This classification is determined by the coefficients of the equations and reflects the geometric relationship between the lines they represent. The determinant of the coefficient matrix plays a crucial role in determining the solution type: a non-zero determinant indicates a unique solution, while a zero determinant suggests either no solution or infinitely many solutions. Real-world applications of systems of equations are vast and varied. In economics, they model supply-demand relationships and market equilibrium. In physics, they describe force balances and motion equations. Engineering uses them for circuit analysis and structural design. The power of systems of equations lies in their ability to model complex relationships and find optimal solutions to real-world problems, making them an essential tool in mathematics and its
